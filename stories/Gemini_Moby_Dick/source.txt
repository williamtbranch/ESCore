One
Say my name is Ishmael. Some years before now—do not think of how much time exactly—I had little or no things to give for other things, and nothing made me want to be on the earth. I thought I would go on the water a little and see the watery part of the world. It is a way I have of making bad feelings go away and making my body feel good. When my mouth is pulled down; when my inside feels like a cold, wet, gray day; when I find my body moving to stop before houses for bodies that do not live, and going behind people taking a body that does not live to the earth; and very much when I feel so bad that I must think very hard to not walk into the road and push things off people's heads—then, I know it is time to go to the sea as soon as I can. I do this so I do not use a stick that makes fire to kill myself. The man Cato pushed his body onto his long stick; I quietly go to the ship. This is not a new or big thing. If they knew it, almost all men, at some time, feel very much the same way about the sea as I do.
Look at the place of the Manhattoes. It is a place of earth with water on all sides. Men doing things to get more things are all around it. Right and left, the roads take you to the water. At the far end is the Battery, where a big stone wall is touched by the water, and cold wind comes from a place far from the earth. Look at all the people there looking at the water.
Walk around the place on a day when people do not do things for other people. Go from Corlears Hook to Coenties Slip, and from there to Whitehall and to the right. What do you see? Standing like men watching all around the place, are many, many men looking at the sea and thinking. Some put their bodies against the big sticks of wood; some sit near the water; some look over the sides of ships from China; some go high up, as if wanting to see the water better. But these are all men of the earth; on other days they are kept inside houses—they must stand at tables, or sit at small tables to do things. How is this? Is the green earth gone? What do they do here?
But look! More people come, walking straight to the water, like they want to fall in. It is not like other things! Nothing makes them feel good but the very end of the earth; standing near the big houses is not enough. No. They must get as near to the water as they can without falling in. And there they stand—so many of them. All are men from the inside of the land, they come from small roads and big roads. But here they all come together. Tell me, does the small piece of stone on those ships that shows the way pull them here?
Say this again. Say you are in a place far from the city; in some high place of lakes. Take almost any way you want, and it will take you down, and leave you there by a little water. There is something you cannot know in it. Take a man who is thinking very deep thoughts—make him stand up, make his feet move, and he will take you to water, if there is water in that place. If you ever want water in the big dry American place, try doing this, if you have a man who thinks about the head with you. Yes, as everyone knows, thinking deep thoughts and water are together for all time.
Here is a man who makes things to look at. He wants to make a thing to look at of the most quiet, good, dreamy place of earth and trees in all the valley of the Saco. What is the biggest thing he uses? There stand his trees; and here sleeps his grass, and there sleep his animals; and up from a small house goes sleep-smoke. A small road goes far into the trees, to the sides of mountains in the blue light. But even if the thing to look at is so good, and the tree drops its parts like water from the eyes on the animal-watcher's head, it would all be for nothing, if the animal-watcher's eye was not looking at the water before him. Go to the Prairies in the time of warm sun, when you walk in flowers to your knees for many miles—what one good thing is not there? Water—there is not one small drop of water there! If Niagara was falling sand, would you go a thousand miles to see it? Why did the poor word-maker of Tennessee, when he got a hand full of white stone, think about giving it for a thing to wear, which he wanted very much, or using his white stone to walk to Rockaway Beach? Why is almost every good boy with a good inside him, at some time wanting to go to sea? Why on your first time on a ship, did you feel your body move in a new way, when people told you that you and your ship could not see the earth now? Why did the old Persians think the sea was of the big makers? Why did the Greeks give it its own big maker, brother of Jove? This all means something. And there is more meaning in the story of Narcissus, who, because he could not hold the face he saw in the water, fell into it and died. But we see that same face in all waters and seas. It is the face of the thing in life that we cannot hold; and this is the key to it all.
Now, when I say that I go to sea when my eyes do not see well, and I think too much of the air I take inside my body, I do not mean that I go to sea as a man who just goes on the ship. To be a man who just goes on the ship, you must have a thing for holding things, and that thing is just a bad part if you have nothing in it. Also, men who go on the ship get bad feelings from the sea—they get angry—they do not sleep at night—they do not feel good, much of the time;—no, I never go as a man who just goes on the ship; and I do not go to sea as a Commodore, or a Captain, or a Cook. I give the good names of those places to those who like them. For my part, I hate all good, hard doing of things of every kind. It is as much as I can do to take care of myself, without taking care of ships. And about going as a cook,—even if it is a good thing to be a cook—I never liked putting fire to birds who do not live;—but when the bird is in the fire, and has good things put on it, there is no one who will say more good words about the bird than I will. Because the old Egyptians liked birds and river-horses from the fire so much, you see the bodies of those animals in their big fire-houses, the pyramids.
No, when I go to sea, I go as a simple man who does things on the ship, right at the front, down in the place where the men sleep, or high up on the tall sticks. It is true, they tell me what to do, and make me jump from stick to stick, like a small green animal in the grass. And at first, this kind of thing is not pleasant. It touches one’s feeling of being a good man, very much if you come from an old, big family on the land, the Van Rensselaers, or Randolphs, or Hardicanutes. And more than all, if just before putting your hand into the black water for the ship, you were a man who gives words to boys in a school, making the big boys feel small before you. The move from a school-man to a ship-man is a hard one, I tell you, and it takes much strong thinking from Seneca and the Stoics to make you make your mouth go up and take it. But even this goes away in time.
What of it, if some old ship-man tells me to get a stick and clean the floor? What does that bad thing mean, if you think of it like the New Testament? Do you think the big sky-man Gabriel thinks I am less of a man, because I do what that old man tells me to do right then? Who is not a man who must do things for another? Tell me that. Well, then, however the old ship-men may tell me what to do—however they may hit me with their hands, I have the good feeling of knowing that it is all right; that everybody else gets the same thing—with the body or in the head; and so the big hit is given to all, and all men should touch each other’s backs and feel good.
Again, I always go to sea as a ship-man, because they make a point of giving me things for my doing, but they never give things to men who just go on the ship. The men who go on the ship must give their things. And there is all the difference in the world between giving things and getting things. The act of giving things is maybe the most bad thing that the two people who took fruit from the tree long ago gave to us. But getting things—what is like it? The happy way a man takes things is a big thing to see, when we think that getting much is the start of all bad things on earth, and that a man with much cannot go to the good place in the sky. Ah! how happily we give ourselves to the bad place!
Finally, I always go to sea as a ship-man, because of the good moving of the body and good air at the front of the ship. For in this world, wind hits the face more than it hits the back, so the Commodore at the back gets his air after it comes from the men at the front. He thinks he gets it first; but not so. In much the same way do the common people lead their leaders in many other things, while the leaders do not know it. But why I, after smelling the sea as a ship-man with things to give, should now want to go on a ship to hunt the big sea-animals; the man of the Fates you cannot see, who watches me, and goes behind me, and makes me do things in a way I cannot know—he can answer better than anyone else. And, it is true, my going on this ship to hunt big sea-animals was part of the big plan of Providence made a long time ago. It came in as a short thing to do between bigger things. I think this part of the words for the show must have been like this:
“Big Fight for the Head Man of the United States.
“SHIP GOING FOR BIG SEA ANIMALS BY ONE ISHMAEL.
“MANY MEN DOING BAD THINGS TO BODIES IN AFFGHANISTAN.”
I cannot tell why those men of the show, the Fates, put me down for this small part of a ship going for big sea animals, when other people got big parts in sad times, and short and easy parts in happy times—I cannot tell why this was exactly; but, now that I think of all the things that happened, I think I can see a little into the reasons which were hidden from me, and made me want to do the part I did, and made me think it was what I wanted to do from my own head.
The biggest of these reasons was the very big thought of the big sea-animal himself. Such a big and strange animal made me want to know him. Then the wild and far seas where he rolled his big body; the bad things of the big sea-animal that have no name; these, with all the strange things of a thousand Patagonian sights and sounds, helped to make me want it. With other men, maybe, such things would not make them want to go; but for me, I have a feeling I cannot stop that wants things that are far away. I love to go on seas where people say not to go, and touch the earth on wild sides of the land. Not turning away from what is good, I am quick to see a very bad, scary thing, and could still be friends with it—if they let me—because it is good to be friends with all the people in the place where one sleeps.
Because of these things, then, the ship going for big sea-animals was a good thing; the big doors of the strange world opened, and in the wild thoughts that made me do this, moving two and two into my deepest inside, came long lines of the big sea-animal, and, in the middle of them all, one big white thing like a mountain of snow in the air.
