THE HARDY BOYS

THE TOWER TREASURE

Chapter I:

THE DEMON THAT GOES VERY FAST

"After the help we gave dad in that case of the papers that were not true, I think he will start to think that we could be detectives when we are grown up."

"Why should we not be?

Is he not one of the most known detectives in the country?

And are we not his sons?

If the work was good enough for him to do it, it should be good enough for us."

Two boys with eyes that shined on motorcycles were going very fast along a road next to the water under the sunlight of a spring morning.

It was Saturday and they were having a good time on a free day from the Bayport school.

The day was very good for a motorcycle trip and the boys were doing a job while they were having a good time by going to do something in a nearby village for their father.

The older of the two boys was a tall young man with dark hair, about sixteen years of age.

His name was Frank Hardy.

The other boy, the one who went with him on the motorcycle trip, was his brother Joe, a year younger.

Although they looked a little the same, more than anything in the strong but happy shape of their mouths, in some things they looked very different.

While Frank was dark, with straight black hair and brown eyes, his brother had a pink face, with light curly hair and blue eyes.

These were the Hardy boys, sons of Fenton Hardy, a detective known in all the countries who had become very known in the years he had spent in the New York police and who now, at the age of forty years, was doing his own work.

The Hardy family lived in Bayport, a city of about fifty thousand people, that was on the Barmet Bay, three miles from the Atlantic, and here the Hardy boys went to school and thought a lot about the days when they, too, would be detectives like their father.

While they went very fast along the not wide road next to the water, with the water hitting on the stones far below, they talked about whether they could make their parents say yes to their big wish to do the same work as their father.

Like most of the children, they talked a lot about the work they should do when they were grown up, and it had always seemed to them that nothing gave so many new and big things to feel as the life of a detective.

"But every time we tell this to dad, he just laughs at us," said Joe Hardy.

"He tells us to wait until we finish school and that after that we can think about being detectives."

"Well, at least he makes us feel that we can do it more than mom does," said Frank.

"She talks very clear and says that she wants one of us to be a doctor and the other to be a lawyer."

"What a good lawyer either of us would be!" said Joe.

"Or a doctor, either!

The two of us were made to be detectives and dad knows it."

"Like I was saying, the help we gave him in that case of the false papers shows that it is true.

He did not say much, but I am sure that he has been thinking a lot."

"Of course we really did not do much in that case," said Joe.

"But we gave the idea of something that led to a clue, is that not true?

That is as big a part of the detective work as anything else.

Dad himself said that it was true that he never would have thought about looking very closely at the money papers of the city to look for that name written by another.

It was just a good luck idea on our part, but it showed him that we can use our heads for something more than to put our hats on."

"Oh, I think he believes it for real.

Once we get out of school, it is very possible that he will let us do it.

Well, this is a good sign right now, is it not?

He asked us to take these papers for him to Willowville.

He is letting us help him."

"I would rather be part of a very good and real mystery," said Frank.

"It is fine to help dad, but if there is nothing in this that makes us feel more alive than taking papers, I would rather start to learn to be a lawyer and finish with that."

"Do not feel bad, Frank," said his brother to make him feel better.

"It may be that we have a mystery that is only ours to find the truth some day."

"If we do, we will show that the sons of Fenton Hardy are good enough to have his name."

Oh man, but what would I not give to be as known by everyone as dad!

Well, some of the biggest cases in the country are given to him.

That case of the false papers, for example.

Fifty thousand dollars had been stolen right in front of the faces of the important men of the city, and all the men who look at the money and the detectives of the city and the private detectives that they called had to say that it was too hard for them."

"Then they called dad and he found the truth in three days.

Once he started to think badly of that man of the numbers who seemed so good, of whom no one had been thinking badly at all, then almost everything was finished.

He made him tell the truth of what he did and all."

"It was a job very well done.

I am happy that the idea we gave him helped him.

The case for sure made a lot of people look at him in the newspapers."

"And here we are," said Joe, "going along the road next to the water on a small and unimportant trip to take some papers of the law to Willowville.

I would rather be looking for some men who steal diamonds or for men who pass things in secret, or something like that."

"Well, we have to be happy with what we have, I suppose," answered Frank, putting his body more forward over the handlebar.

"Maybe dad can give us the chance to be in a real case some day."

"Some day!

I want to be in a real case now!

The motorcycles made a very big noise along the not wide road that went along the side of the bay.

One side of land with big and small fallen stones went down very fast toward the water below, and on the other side of the road there was a very high cliff.

The road itself was not wide, although it was wide enough to let two cars meet and pass, and it gave many turns and changed sides many times.

It was a road where the people did not go much, because Willowville was only a small village and this coast road was a part that came out of the bigger roads toward the north and the west.

The Hardy boys stopped talking about the idea that it was possible that some day they would become detectives, and for a time they kept moving without saying anything, putting all their mind on how hard it was to stay on the road.

Because the road at this point was bad, very rough and with many holes, and went up very fast so that the side of land that went down to the ocean far below became higher and harder every time.

"I would not like to fall down the side around here," said Frank, while he looked down at the hard land that went down.

"It is a fall of a hundred feet.

You would break in many parts before you touched the land below."

"It is true!

It is better to stay near the cliff.

These turns are very bad."

The motorcycles passed the next turn very well, and then the boys had in front of them a long land that went up very fast.

The stone cliffs looked big and dark on one side, and the side of land went down a lot to the waves that moved hard below, so that the road was only a very small line in front of them.

"Once we get to the highest part of the land we will be fine.

It is very easy to go from there to Willowville," said Frank, while the motorcycles started to go up.

In that moment, over the fast noise of their own motors, they heard the very loud and high noise of a car that was coming very fast.

The car still could not be seen, but it was very sure that it was moving making all the noise possible and without thinking about the rules of not going so fast.

"What a foolish person is driving like this on this kind of road!" said Frank loudly.

They looked back.

Even while he was talking, the car appeared very fast before their eyes.

It came around the turn behind, and so fast did the driver take the bad turn that two wheels were without touching the ground while the car appeared very fast before their eyes.

A cloud of dust and stones rose up, the car moved with a lot of force from left to right, and then it made a big noise moving very fast down the road.

The boys saw for a moment a person who looked very hard at the wheel.

How he kept the car on the road was something that could not be believed, because the car that was going very fast moved from one side to the other.

In one moment it would be in a big danger of falling over the edge of the road, toward the rocks below. In the moment that followed, the car would be on the other side of the road, touching the cliff a little.

"He will pass over us!" shouted Joe, with a lot of fear.

"The foolish man!"

For real, the place where the two boys were was full of danger.

The road was quite small from side to side at any moment, and this car that was going very fast was taking every part of the space.

In a big cloud of dust, it went straight toward the two motorcyclists.

It seemed to jump through the air.

The front wheels came out of a long hole in the ground, and the back part of the car moved to the side with a lot of force.

With one turn of the wheel, the driver made the car come back to the road again, just when it seemed that it was going to fall over the edge of the road.

It went very fast toward the cliff, moved to the side to come back to the middle of the road, and then went forward at a very big speed.

Frank and Joe moved their motorcycles little by little as far to the right of the road as they could without feeling too much fear.

With a lot of fear, they saw that the car was moving to the side without control again.

The driver did nothing to go less fast.

The car came moving very fast and without control toward them!
