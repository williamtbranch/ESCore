Chapter 2. The Travel Bag.

I put a few things in my old travel bag, put it under my arm and went out toward Cape Horn and the Pacific. Leaving the good city of Manhattan, which is a place with many people and a lot of noise, I arrived at the port of New Bedford. It was a Saturday night, in the month of December. I felt very sad to learn that the small boat for Nantucket had already left, and that there would be no other way of reaching that place until the following Monday.

As most young men who want to go hunt whales stay in New Bedford to begin their voyage, I must say that I had no intentions of doing that. My mind was decided to sail only on a boat from Nantucket, because there was something big and free in everything related to that famous old land, which pleased me very much. Although New Bedford has been winning the whale hunt and poor Nantucket has stayed behind, Nantucket was the great mother of all, the first place where a great dead whale reached American land. From what other place but Nantucket did the first men go out in their wooden boats to search for the great whale of the sea?

%%META illustration: ch02_swordfish_inn%%
Now, having a night, a day, and another entire night ahead of me in New Bedford before being able to go up onto the ship, I thought a lot about where I was going to eat and rest during all that time. It was a very dark night, sad, and with a very great cold. I did not know anyone in that place. I searched my pocket slowly and only found a few silver coins. 'Well, Ishmael,' I said to myself while I stood in the middle of a cold road with my bag on my shoulder, 'in any place where you decide to stay tonight, be sure to ask the price first and look for a place that does not cost much money.'

I walked step by step along the roads and passed in front of a house that had a sign that said 'The Crossed Harpoons,' but it seemed that they charged a lot of money there. Further on, I saw the bright windows of another house called 'The Swordfish.' From there came such a good light that it seemed that the cold was leaving in front of the door, while in all other places the cold ice covered the road. I was very tired and my boots were broken from long use. 'There too they are going to ask for a lot of money,' I thought again, stopping a moment to look at the light and hear the sound of the glasses inside. 'But go on, Ishmael,' I said to myself at last. 'Your old broken boots are in the middle of the road.' So I kept walking. I followed the roads that went toward the water, because there without a doubt would be the cheapest houses.

%%META illustration: ch02_the_trap%%
What sad roads! They were like big blocks of darkness, not houses, on each side, and from time to time one saw a small candle, like a candle moving inside a tomb. At that hour of the night, the last day of the week, that place seemed empty. But suddenly I saw a light with a little smoke coming out of a low and wide house, where the door was not closed. It seemed like a place for everyone, so I entered, but the first thing I did was fall over a box of gray dust at the door. 'Oh!' I thought while the dust almost did not let me breathe, 'could this be the place of fire that the Bible speaks of?' The dust made me feel bad, but I heard someone who spoke with a lot of strength inside, opened the second door and entered.

It seemed like a great meeting of shadows. A hundred dark men turned to look at me, and beyond, a black man spoke with strength from a high place. It was a house of God for those people, and the words that the man said were about the darkness of death and the pain of those who feel bad there. 'Oh, Ishmael!' I said to myself, leaving in a hurry. 'What a bad place to stay!'

%%META illustration: ch02_spouter_inn_sign%%
Walking again, at last I arrived at a small light near the port, and heard a sad creaking of wood. Looking up, I saw a sign that moved over the door, with a white painting that showed a lot of water that came out with strength, and these words below: 'The Whale Tavern: Peter Coffin.'

'Peter Coffin?' I thought. Coffin means box for the dead in another tongue, which did not seem like a good idea at that moment. But I told myself that it is a common name in Nantucket, and that this Peter must be a man from there. Since the light looked small, the place seemed quite quiet, the old wooden house looked very old and the wooden sign made a sad noise as it moved with the wind, I thought that this was the best place to sleep cheap and drink a hot coffee.

It was a strange and rare new place, an old house with a high roof, which seemed to be falling toward one side. It was on a cold corner, where the strong wind made a lot of noise, worse than the wind that moved Saint Paul's ship in the Bible. But that cold wind is very pleasant for one who is inside, with the body near the fire before going to sleep. There is a big difference between looking at the cold wind from a clean window where the ice is only on the outside, or looking at it from a window without glass, where the ice is on the inside and on the outside, and where death is the only one that lives in the house. It is true, I thought, while I remembered these words. This body of mine is like a house, and my eyes are its windows. What a pity that they did not close well the small gaps of this house before finishing it, but now it is too late to make changes. The world is already finished, and we only live in it.

But what does the poor man think who is cold on the road? Can he perhaps give warmth to his hands looking at the beautiful lights of the night? Would he not prefer to be in a warm land of sun instead of this cold? Would he not want to go straight to a place of fire so as not to feel this ice in the body?

But let us not talk more of these sad things, because we are going to hunt whales and there will already be enough of that later on. Let us remove the ice from our cold feet and see what kind of place this tavern is.
