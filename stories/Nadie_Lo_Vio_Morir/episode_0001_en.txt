He's Not Daniel
Episode One

Far from the big cities there is a small town. The people who live there know one another well; everyone knows who each person is. Nothing big ever happens, and that's how they like it. In that town lives Rosa. She lives with Inés, an older woman who has seen many years and knows many things — perhaps more than people think.

A long time ago, in that same town, there lived a man: Daniel. Rosa was always with him. When she was at his side she felt good, and she wanted to be with him all her life. But Daniel wanted to see the big cities and do big things, and one day he left. A long time passed. Then some words came to the town: Daniel has died. Rosa heard them, and something inside her died too.

Now, one day, someone arrives in the town. The people see him and can't speak. He's just like Daniel. He walks the way Daniel walked. When they ask him: "Daniel? Are you Daniel?", he says: "No. I'm not Daniel. I'm Marco."

Inés sees Marco from far away, and she feels something very bad. She says to Rosa: "Listen carefully. Don't go near that man. Don't talk to him. I know something about him, something from a long time ago. If you knew it, you couldn't live the way you did before." Rosa wants to know, but Inés only says: "No. There are things it's better not to know. I'm not going to say any more."

That night, when everyone is inside their houses, Marco comes up to Rosa's house. He speaks to her in a low voice. He says words that only Daniel knew. Words that Daniel said to her a long time ago, words she never told anyone. Rosa can't move. She thinks: People say Daniel has died. But did anyone see him die? Then Marco says: "You know who I am. And maybe you know why I'm here."

After that night, Rosa can't be at peace. She doesn't want to see anyone, doesn't want to hear anyone. Marco's words move inside her all the time, like something alive. She thinks: If this man isn't Daniel, how does he know those words? And if he is Daniel, why does he say he isn't? In the end she knows one thing: she has to see him. She has to hear more.

Rosa goes to find Marco. She says to him: "Only one person knew those words, and they say that person is dead. Who are you?" Marco looks at her and says nothing for a while. Then he says: "Maybe a person can be dead and not dead. Maybe a person dies because others want him dead. Maybe he dies because someone says 'he's dead,' and everyone believes it." Rosa says: "I don't understand. Speak plainly." And Marco says: "Not now. Maybe later. Right now it's not good for you to know."

In the town lives Tomás. When Daniel was a boy, Tomás was always with him; the two were like a single person, and people said: where one is, the other is. When the words came to the town a long time ago — Daniel has died — it was Tomás who said them before anyone else. He said: "I was far away, in a big city, and they told me there." Now Tomás sees Marco, and he feels something very bad inside. Not because Marco is just like Daniel. It's because of something Tomás did a long time ago, something no one in the town knows.

That same day, Tomás goes to see Inés. He says to her: "You see what I see. If that man is Daniel, you know what could happen to me." Inés says: "I know what you did, Tomás. I knew it before all of this. But listen: I don't want you to hurt that man. If you hurt him now, everyone will know what you did." Tomás says: "And if I do nothing?" Inés says nothing more.

That night, Inés goes to see Marco. She walks slowly, like a woman who has lived many years. She says: "A long time ago you told me: I'm never coming back to this town. For years I said nothing to anyone. Why have you come back now?" And Marco says: "Because now I know what happened. I know what Tomás did. And I know, Inés, that you saw it all, and you said nothing."

Rosa sees Inés as she comes back from seeing Marco. She sees something in her: Inés walks like someone carrying something very heavy inside. Rosa comes up to her and says: "You went to see him. I saw you. What did he say to you?" Inés doesn't want to talk. But Rosa goes on: "You know things, Inés. You know things about Marco. You know things about Daniel. And you tell me nothing. I'm not a child anymore. I want to know the truth."

Inés looks at her for a long while. Then she says: "There are truths that hurt, Rosa. Once you hear them, you can't live the way you did before. Your life is one thing now; after you know, it's another. Is that what you want?" And Rosa says: "Yes. I want it. Because living like this — not knowing, thinking all the time, feeling this inside — is like being dead. I want the truth, Inés. I want it more than anything." Inés says in a low voice: "You're like him. Daniel used to say things like that too." After a moment, she says more: "I'm going to tell you one thing. One thing only, no more. When Daniel left the town, he didn't leave alone. Tomás left with him. The two of them left the same night. And a long time later, Tomás came back here alone." Rosa can't move. She says: "But Tomás says he was far away, and that the people of a big city told him that Daniel…" "Yes," says Inés. "That's what he says." And she says no more.

Rosa thinks a great deal about Inés's words. The two of them left the same night. Tomás came back alone. And it was Tomás who said: Daniel has died. She thinks: I want to hear what Tomás says. I want to see his face when he speaks.

Rosa goes to Tomás's house. She says to him: "Tomás, you said those words a long time ago. You said: Daniel has died. How did you know? Who told you? Did you see his body?" Tomás isn't looking at her when he speaks. He's looking at something else, something far away, something that isn't here. He says: "I was far away, in a big city. They told me there. I didn't see his body. But I know it's true." Rosa says: "How can you know it's true, if you didn't see it?" And Tomás says, almost shouting: "Because I know it! People don't say things like that if they aren't true!" But his face says something else. His hands tremble; he can't stay still. Rosa sees this and thinks: You left with him, Tomás. Inés knows it. I know it. Why are you saying this? But she says nothing. Something inside her says: not now.

Another night, Tomás goes to see Marco. He goes like someone who doesn't want to be seen. Marco is there, like someone who knows who's coming before he sees them. Tomás says: "Why did you come to this town? What do you want?" Marco doesn't move. He says: "I want to see this town. I want to see the people. I want to see how a man lives when he thinks: no one knows what I did." Tomás feels something very bad inside. He says: "If you're Daniel, say it now." And Marco says: "And if I am? What do you do then, Tomás? The same thing you did before?"

Tomás says nothing. He can't. Marco comes closer and says in a low voice: "I know what happened in the city. I know what you did. And I want this: you tell it to all the people of the town. You yourself. If you don't say it, I will. You don't have much time." Tomás goes back to his house walking like a dead man.

After that, Tomás can't be at peace. He thinks one thing, and thinks it all the time: That man knows. How can he know? Only two people knew what happened: me and… No. It can't be. The dead don't come back. The dead don't speak. The dead don't know. But he also thinks another thing, and that thing is like something alive inside him: Did anyone see him die? No one. No one saw him die.

Rosa can't stay in her house. She thinks: Maybe now I can see him. Maybe now he'll tell me more. She goes to find Marco. He sees her coming, and for a moment — a very small moment — he looks at her the way Daniel used to look at her a long time ago. She sees it. She sees it and feels something very big inside.

She says to him: "When you saw me just now, something happened inside you. I saw it. Daniel used to look at me like that. Who are you? Say it. If you're Daniel, say it to me. Not to the people: to me." Marco says nothing for a long while. Then he says: "If I say 'I'm Daniel,' what happens? You want your Daniel to live. But the Daniel you want is dead, Rosa. He died when he left the town. The man who's here now knows things that that Daniel didn't know. He saw things that that Daniel didn't see. A person can die and go on living, and not be the same."

Rosa says: "I don't want any more words. Just tell me one thing: yes or no." And Marco looks at her — looks at her the way Daniel used to a long time ago — and says: "I want this: that all the people learn the truth about Tomás. After that, you'll know the truth about me. The two truths are parts of one single thing."

Marco says no more, and Rosa goes back to her house. The night is over the whole town. And inside her, all the time, the words she can't stop thinking move: People say Daniel has died. But no one saw him die. No one.

*(Here Episode One ends. There is more after.)*
